## SCRIPT 1 — "How to Launch a Business in South Africa in 2026"
**Tagline: The complete A-Z guide — from idea to first revenue**
**Suggested title: "I Built a Company in South Africa. Here's Everything I Learned."**

---

### INTRO — (0:00-1:00)
*[Scene: Tumelo at desk, talking to camera. Or: animated intro with title card]*

"Welcome. Today I'm going to tell you exactly how I built a company in South Africa — from scratch, without connections, without a trust fund, and without a fancy overseas degree from a top-tier university."

"I started with a butcher's shop. Now I'm running a group of companies that spans meat distribution, pharmaceutical logistics, AI infrastructure, and global trade. And I'm going to walk you through every single step — the legal structure, the funding, the mistakes, the wins, all of it."

"If you've ever thought about starting a business in South Africa, this video is for you. If you're already building and you've hit a wall, this is also for you. Let's go."

---

### SECTION 1 — The Idea Phase (1:00-3:00)
*[Scene: Tumelo talking, or: text on screen with key points]*

"Step one: find a real problem. Not a solution looking for a problem — an actual pain point that people will pay money to have removed."

"In my case, it was simple. South Africans who wanted premium meat — Wagyu beef, Ankole cattle, artisanal biltong — had two options: expensive imports, or low-quality local mass production. There was nobody doing premium local. So I did."

"Your first exercise is this: write down three problems you have that you would pay to have solved. Those are your business ideas. Now ask yourself: is this a pain point that a large enough market has? Because a solution to a niche of ten people is a hobby, not a business."

"Once you have the problem, test it. Before you register a company, before you spend a cent — go talk to ten people who have this problem. Ask them if they'd pay for your solution. Not if they'd use it — if they'd pay. There's a huge difference."

---

### SECTION 2 — The Legal Structure (3:00-5:30)
*[Scene: Screen recording of CIPC registration, or: talking to camera]*

"Step two: structure your business correctly from day one. In South Africa, this matters more than most people realize."

"The three main options: a sole proprietorship, a close corporation, and a private company. A sole proprietorship is the fastest to set up — you can do it in a day on the CIPC website. But it has one critical flaw: you're personally liable for everything. If your business gets sued, your house is on the line."

"A private company — Pty Ltd — costs about R1,250 to register and gives you a separate legal identity. This means your personal assets are protected. It also means you can raise investment, open a business bank account properly, and sign contracts in the company's name."

"My advice: register your Pty Ltd on the CIPC's eServices portal. It takes two to five working days. Then get a tax number from SARS — eFiling, not the branch, it takes an hour. Then register for VAT if you expect to exceed R1 million in annual revenue."

"Don't forget: B-BBEE certification. Even if you're not targeting government contracts yet, get your B-BBEE certificate from aSANAS-accredited agency. It's the difference between a R50,000 contract and a R5 million contract in South Africa."

---

### SECTION 3 — Funding (5:30-8:00)
*[Scene: Tumelo talking, or: graphics with funding options]*

"Here's the part everyone gets stuck on: money."

"In South Africa, there are five real funding paths. Let me break each one down honestly."

"First: self-funding. Bootstrapping. I did this for the first two years. It means you keep your day job, you work evenings and weekends, you spend your own savings. It sucks. But it means you own 100% of your company and you make decisions based on reality, not on investor expectations."

"Second: the Informal Market. Friends, family, and fools. Tell everyone you know what you're building. Someone will write you a R5,000 or R20,000 cheque. It won't come with term sheets or board seats. It will come with expectations. Be clear about those upfront."

"Third: grants. The Technology Innovation Agency. The Small Enterprise Development Agency. The Jobs Fund. These are non-dilutive — you don't give up equity. They are slow and bureaucratic. But they exist, and many entrepreneurs don't even try them."

"Fourth: angel investors and venture capital. In South Africa, there's a growing early-stage ecosystem. Angels typically write R250,000 to R2 million cheques for 10-20% of your company. VCs write larger amounts for faster-growing businesses. But they want to see traction: revenue, users, growth."

"Fifth: debt. Banks don't love lending to early-stage businesses without collateral. But if you have assets — equipment, property, inventory — an asset-backed loan is possible. At Studex, we used our cold-chain infrastructure as collateral for our first serious expansion loan."

---

### SECTION 4 — The First Revenue (8:00-10:00)
*[Scene: Tumelo talking about early customers]*

"Revenue is the only metric that matters at the beginning. Not followers. Not downloads. Not brand awareness. Revenue. Money in the bank."

"My first paying customer for Studex Meat came from a Facebook post. I photographed our Wagyu beef, wrote three sentences about it, and posted it to a food group. Someone replied within twenty minutes. That was R800 for a single order."

"You don't need a website. You don't need an app. You need to sell something, to someone, for money, right now. Everything else — the systems, the branding, the team — comes after revenue proves the concept."

"Get your first R10,000 in sales before you write a business plan. Get your first R100,000 before you raise money. Get your first R1 million before you think about scaling."

---

### SECTION 5 — Building the Team (10:00-12:00)
*[Scene: Tumelo talking, team photos in background]*

"At some point, you can't do it alone anymore. And this is where most founders make the mistake of hiring too early."

"The rule I follow: don't hire until the work is so urgent and so repetitive that it is actively costing you money to do it yourself. Not when you feel overwhelmed — when you have proof that your time is worth more than the salary you're about to pay."

"And when you do hire: hire for character and train for skill. The best employees I've ever had weren't the most experienced. They were the most hungry. They wanted to learn, they took responsibility, they showed up early and stayed late. Skills can be taught. Initiative cannot."

---

### CLOSING (12:00-13:00)
*[Scene: Tumelo looking at camera, direct]*

"So here's the entire playbook in thirty seconds. Find a real problem. Register a Pty Ltd. Talk to ten potential customers before you build anything. Get your first revenue before you raise money. Hire only when the work is costing you money to delay."

"The rest is just execution. And execution is just showing up, every day, and making decisions. Good decisions compound. Bad decisions compound too — but in the wrong direction."

"If you take one thing from this video: stop waiting for the perfect moment. The moment is now. Go build."

"If this was useful, subscribe. I'll see you in the next one."

## SCRIPT 2 — "The AI Opportunity for African Entrepreneurs"
**Tagline: Why Africa is the biggest untapped AI market in the world**
**Suggested title: "Why Every African Entrepreneur Should Be Using AI Right Now"**

---

### INTRO — (0:00-1:00)
*[Scene: Tumelo at desk, AI tools on screen]*

"Let me tell you something that most people in Africa haven't figured out yet. AI is not a threat. It is the single greatest equalizer that has ever existed for African entrepreneurs."

"Why do I say that? Because AI makes the cost of expertise nearly zero. Before AI, if you wanted to run a marketing campaign, you needed a marketing team. If you wanted to analyze your financials, you needed an accountant. If you wanted to build software, you needed a developer."

"Now? One person with the right AI tools can do the work of an entire team. And that changes everything for African businesses, where hiring specialized talent is expensive and often unavailable."

"In this video, I'm going to show you exactly how I'm using AI at Studex — in our operations, our marketing, our product development. And I'm going to give you a framework for finding your own AI opportunities. Stay to the end — there's something most people miss."

---

### SECTION 1 — The AI Landscape in 2026 (1:00-3:30)
*[Scene: Tumelo talking, with tools visible on screen]*

"Before we get into the opportunities, let's be clear on what's actually available right now."

"For language and writing: GPT-4 from OpenAI and Claude from Anthropic are the gold standard. Both can write better than most professionals. Both are available via API, which means you can build them into your products."

"For coding: GitHub Copilot and Cursor are extraordinary. If you have an idea for a software product, you can now build an MVP in a weekend with AI assistance. What used to take a team of five developers now takes one person and AI."

"For voice and video: Eleven Labs for voice cloning and speech synthesis. MiniMax for video generation. Runway for video editing. The barrier to producing professional audio and video content has dropped to nearly zero."

"For local deployment: Ollama lets you run powerful AI models — including Meta's Llama and Alibaba's Qwen — on your own hardware. No API calls. No data leaving your server. This is critical for businesses with privacy requirements."

"And here's what's new in 2026: AI agents. Not AI tools — AI agents. A tool answers your questions. An agent takes actions. It can browse the web, send emails, write code, manage your calendar, run your social media. It works while you sleep."

---

### SECTION 2 — The African Opportunity (3:30-6:00)
*[Scene: Tumelo talking, map of Africa in background]*

"Now here's what most people miss. The AI opportunity in Africa is not the same as the AI opportunity in the US or Europe. It is actually bigger."

"Why? Because the existing infrastructure is weaker. In the US, businesses have had decades to build systems, hire staff, develop processes. In Africa, many of those systems don't exist yet. That means AI doesn't have to compete with an entrenched status quo — it can define the baseline."

"Consider this: the average South African small business does not have a dedicated marketing person, a dedicated accountant, a dedicated IT person, or a dedicated customer service team. Now ask: what does an AI agent do? It does all four. At once. For the cost of a monthly subscription."

"The markets where AI has the biggest impact are the markets with the least existing infrastructure. Africa qualifies. Specifically: financial services for the unbanked. Agricultural supply chains. Healthcare triage. Education. Logistics. These are all industries where AI can provide more value in Africa than in developed markets, precisely because the alternative — nothing — is worse."

"And then there's the language opportunity. Africa has over 2,000 languages. Global AI companies have not built for this. A company that builds AI-powered tools for Yoruba, isiZulu, isiXhosa, Setswana, and the other official languages of Africa — that company has a defensible moat that the global players cannot replicate quickly."

---

### SECTION 3 — How Studex Uses AI (6:00-9:00)
*[Scene: Tumelo talking, AI tools/dashboards visible]*

"Let me be specific about how we use AI at Studex. This is not theoretical. This is production."

"First: our AI agents. We have a team of five agents, each with a specific role. Hermes manages our technical infrastructure — GitHub, deployment pipelines, cloud servers. Naledi manages our social media — twenty posts a month across Meta, Instagram, and LinkedIn, all scheduled and drafted by AI. Robusca is our chief of staff — coordinating priorities, managing our operational dashboard."

"Second: our Obsidian Mind system. Every client conversation, every deal, every decision — stored permanently in a memory vault. When Naledi drafts a social media post about Pharmasyntez, she reads the Pharmasyntez context first. When Hermes reviews our code, he reads the previous sprint context. The agents don't start from zero."

"Third: voice AI. We have a voice agent that speaks to our clients in real time. It qualifies leads, answers common questions, and books appointments. It runs on our own VM in Johannesburg. The call data never leaves South Africa."

"Fourth: web automation. Our Page-Agent reads government tender websites, extracts new opportunities, and updates our pipeline tracker automatically. What used to take a person two hours a day now happens continuously in the background."

"And fifth: we're building this as a product. Other African businesses don't have access to these tools yet. We are building the infrastructure so they can plug in. That's the business model. And that's why it's a large opportunity."

---

### SECTION 4 — The Framework (9:00-11:00)
*[Scene: Tumelo talking, steps on screen]*

"Here's a framework you can use today. I call it the AI Leverage test."

"Take any task you do in your business. Write it down. Now ask: could an AI agent do this task in less than thirty seconds? If yes — automate it immediately. That's leverage you're leaving on the table."

"The tasks that survive this filter — the ones where AI can't do it yet — those are your high-value activities. Those are where you should spend your time. Not on admin, not on data entry, not on scheduling — on the things that require your specific judgment, your relationships, your creativity."

"And then ask a second question: can AI amplify this? Can AI make me better at this task? For example: I use AI to draft the first version of proposals. I then edit and refine them. AI doesn't replace my judgment on strategy — it gets me to a first draft in twenty minutes instead of three hours. That's amplification."

"Start with one tool. Not five tools, not ten tools. One. For most people: start with Claude or ChatGPT. Use it for writing first — emails, proposals, reports. Once you're comfortable with that, expand. Add a second tool. Then a third."

"The entrepreneurs who wait until AI is 'ready' will be the last to benefit. The ones using AI today are building the habits, the workflows, and the competitive advantages that will be very difficult to displace in five years."

---

### CLOSING (11:00-12:00)
*[Scene: Tumelo, direct to camera]*

"The question is not whether AI will change your industry. It will. The question is whether you'll be the one driving that change, or whether you'll be the one it happens to."

"For African entrepreneurs especially, AI is not a luxury. It is a survival tool. It is the only way a small team in Johannesburg can compete with a large corporation that has a hundred employees. Not by having more people — by having smarter systems."

"Go use one AI tool today. Just one. Write one proposal with AI. Send one email with AI. Automate one repetitive task with AI. And then come back tomorrow and do another one."

"Subscribe. There's a lot more to come."

## SCRIPT 3 — "How to Build a Global Business from Africa"
**Tagline: Studex's journey from local butcher to Africa-Russia trade corridor**
**Suggested title: "From a Butcher's Shop to a Global Trade Platform — My Journey"**

---

### INTRO — (0:00-1:30)
*[Scene: Tumelo walking through a premium butchery or meat counter. Or: talking directly to camera]*

"This piece of meat — right here — started a technology company."

"I'm going to tell you the real story. Not the polished version, not the version I tell investors. The real story. Including the parts where I failed, the decisions I got wrong, and the moments I almost gave up."

"My name is Tumelo Ramaphosa. I started Studex Meat — a premium butchery in Cape Town. Wagyu beef, Ankole cattle, artisanal biltong. We sold to restaurants, hotels, and directly to consumers who cared about quality."

"Today, Studex Group spans three businesses. Studex Meat — the product brand. Studex Global Markets — the Africa-Russia trade and technology ecosystem. And Studex Health — which we are building right now with one of Russia's largest pharmaceutical manufacturers."

"And I'm going to tell you exactly how we got here. And more importantly — I'm going to tell you how you can do the same thing."

---

### CHAPTER 1 — The Beginning (1:30-4:30)
*[Scene: Tumelo talking, old photos if available]*

"I started the way most African entrepreneurs start: with nothing, and with everything to prove."

"In 2014, I was working in a corporate job. Good salary. Stable. Respectable. And I was completely bored. I knew I was going to start something. I just didn't know what."

"The insight came from my grandmother's kitchen. She made the best biltong I'd ever tasted. And I realized: she had a recipe, a process, that had been passed down through generations. But nobody in South Africa had turned that into a premium food brand. It was either cheap supermarket biltong, or expensive imported specialty products."

"I quit my job on a Friday. By the following Monday, I was at a butchery supplier, buying my first stock on credit. Total investment: R8,000 — most of my savings."

"The first six months were brutal. I was working eighteen-hour days. Selling at a Saturday market. Delivering orders from the back of my car. Sleeping on the floor of the storage unit when I couldn't afford rent."

"But the product was good. Really good. People came back. And slowly, the revenue started to grow."

---

### CHAPTER 2 — The Pivot That Changed Everything (4:30-7:30)
*[Scene: Tumelo talking, trade event photos]*

"By 2018, Studex Meat was profitable. Not wealthy — profitable. Enough to pay me a salary and keep the lights on."

"And then something happened that I didn't expect. A Russian businessman came into the market. He was looking for a South African partner to distribute pharmaceutical products into Africa. He had the manufacturing. He needed local expertise, local relationships, and local infrastructure."

"I said no at first. I'm a meat guy. What do I know about pharmaceuticals?"

"But the more I thought about it, the more I realized: this is exactly what I had been building toward. The ability to move products across borders. The relationships with logistics providers. The understanding of South African regulatory requirements. The cold-chain infrastructure."

"And it hit me: I had been building a trade business without knowing it. The meat was just the first product."

"I said yes. And that single decision — saying yes to a conversation I almost turned down — became the foundation of everything that came after."

---

### CHAPTER 3 — The Ecosystem Play (7:30-10:30)
*[Scene: Tumelo talking, platform diagram visible]*

"Here's what I learned from that first partnership: the most valuable businesses are not product businesses. They are infrastructure businesses."

"A product business sells a thing. An infrastructure business sits in the middle of a market and makes every transaction flow through it. Amazon doesn't make most of the products it sells — it makes the marketplace. Uber doesn't own most of the cars — it owns the platform."

"So I started building infrastructure. Not for one industry — for the Africa-Russia trade corridor."

"We built the compliance layer — understanding SAHPRA requirements for pharmaceuticals, understanding the regulatory frameworks for different African countries. We built the logistics layer — cold-chain infrastructure that could handle temperature-sensitive products across multiple borders. We built the AI layer — agents that could manage documentation, track shipments, and handle communications."

"And then we opened that infrastructure to other companies. AfricaBiz for trade facilitation. NtechLab for AI and facial recognition technology. ART Engineering for manufacturing. Pharmasyntez for pharmaceutical distribution. Each company brings a different capability to the network. Each company makes the whole network stronger."

"That's called a keiretsu — a Japanese concept for a network of companies bound together by shared interest. And it is the most defensible business structure I know."

---

### CHAPTER 4 — The Technology Layer (10:30-13:00)
*[Scene: Tumelo at desk with AI dashboard visible]*

"Now here's where it gets interesting. In 2025, I started building AI agents into every part of the business."

"I didn't do this because it was fashionable. I did it because we had a problem. We were growing too fast for our team to keep up. Documents were getting lost. Communications were falling through the cracks. Opportunities were being missed because nobody had bandwidth to follow up."

"So I built a team of AI agents. Five of them. Each with a specific role. They work around the clock. They don't get tired. They don't make mistakes from fatigue. And they compound — meaning every conversation they have makes them better at the next one."

"The result: we now operate at a level that would have required fifty employees five years ago, with a team of twelve. And we're profitable."

"And I realized something important: this technology — the AI agent infrastructure — is valuable not just for Studex. It's valuable for every African business that doesn't have access to a fifty-person team. It's infrastructure."

"And that's what OGRE Computer is. A technology layer that sits on top of our trade infrastructure and makes it accessible to other businesses."

---

### CHAPTER 5 — What I Would Do Differently (13:00-15:00)
*[Scene: Tumelo talking, reflective tone]*

"If I could start over, here's what I would do differently."

"First: I would build the legal structure correctly from day one. We wasted two years untangling a partnership structure that was set up incorrectly at the start. A Pty Ltd from day one would have saved us enormous pain."

"Second: I would have hired a finance person earlier. I tried to do the finances myself for three years. I'm not a finance person. We lost more money to bad financial decisions than we made in revenue in our first year. Hire what you're not."

"Third: I would have started selling internationally from month one. I waited until year three. By then, competitors had already established themselves. Get to market faster, even if your product isn't perfect."

"Fourth: I would have started building in public earlier. The decisions to document your journey, to build an audience, to tell your story — those compound. By the time I started taking content seriously, I had three years of stories that nobody knew."

"And fifth: I would have built the AI infrastructure from day one. Not because I knew it would work — I didn't. But because the cost of AI tools in 2026 is a fraction of what it was in 2018. The tools that took me years to build internally can now be assembled in weeks."

---

### CLOSING (15:00-16:00)
*[Scene: Tumelo looking directly at camera]*

"So here's what I want you to take away from this."

"Number one: start before you're ready. I started with R8,000 and a recipe for biltong. You don't need more than that."

"Number two: say yes to conversations. The opportunity that changed my business came from a conversation I almost didn't take. Say yes more than you say no."

"Number three: build infrastructure, not just products. The product is how you get in. The infrastructure is how you stay in."

"Number four: use AI now. Not next year. Not when it's cheaper. Now. The competitive advantages you build today will be very hard to displace in five years."

"And number five: Africa is not a limitation. It is the market. The gap between where the infrastructure is and where it needs to be — that's where the opportunities are."

"This piece of meat started a technology company. The next piece of meat could start yours."

"Subscribe. See you in the next one."
